01 — The book

About the game

Tinea is an independent narrative game currently in development.

The story follows a woman who returns to her hometown after the death of her mother. What was supposed to be just a few days organizing an old library gradually becomes a journey through memories, forgotten objects, and small traces left behind by people who passed through that place over decades.

The game uses exploration, environmental storytelling, and subtle psychological suspense to tell a story about memory, grief, and the way people continue to exist through the things they leave behind.

Instead of focusing on combat or constant action, Tinea invites the player to observe, explore, and understand the stories hidden among shelves, photographs, letters, and old books.

Tinea is less about solving a death and more about understanding what remains after someone is gone.

03 — The archive

In Tinea, collectibles are not there only to be found. Each object is a memory, a fragment of someone, a small mark left inside the library.

Old photos with handwritten notes on the back. Some capture simple moments. Others seem to hold something left unsaid.

04 — Philosophy

Small objects, large stories

Tinea believes small objects can tell large stories.

The library as an archive

The library is not only a setting, but an archive of lives, affection, and absence.

Narrative through details

Every photograph, letter, dedication, or annotated book represents a trace left by someone.

No rush

The player is invited to observe, return, reread, and slowly assemble the stories.

Silence also speaks

Not everything needs to be explained in order to be felt. Some memories remain in what was never said.

05 — Atmosphere

Rain, silence, and old paper

Rather than constantly trying to scare the player, Tinea seeks to evoke a lingering feeling of nostalgia, silence, and quiet unease.

The library is both welcoming and unsettling. A place where the scent of old books, rain against the windows, and forgotten objects seem to preserve memories that refuse to disappear.

06 — The uncanny

Not every memory stays the same

In Tinea, suspense does not exist to replace melancholy. It appears quietly, like the feeling that something changed while no one was looking.

Subtle psychological suspense

Discomfort comes more from observation than from direct jumpscares.

A living environment

The library seems to react to what has been seen, read, and remembered.

Unstable memories

Some details may feel different as the protagonist progresses.

Silence as tension

Not everything needs to be explained in order to be felt.

07 — Book of Records

The library keeps receiving visitors

Throughout the game, the Book of Records works as a readable object: an old notebook left inside the library, filled with notes, names, and small events recorded over the years.

By the end of the experience, a new possibility appears. The player may leave a small trace of their own, as if they had also passed through that library.

This idea is still in development, but it comes from the desire to turn the act of playing into one more memory preserved by the space.

08 — Inspirations

Games, feelings, and references

Tinea is inspired by works that tell stories through small details, objects, environments, and silence.

What Remains of Edith Finch

For its environmental storytelling and the way objects and spaces reveal family histories.

Life is Strange

For its contemplative pacing, everyday details, and the emotional weight of photographs, choices, and memories.

To the Moon / A Bird Story

For their relationship between memory, affection, loss, and small scenes capable of carrying deep emotion.

OMORI / GRIS

For their symbolic approach to pain, trauma, grief, reconstruction, and feelings that are difficult to name.

Celeste

For its emotional journey and the idea of transforming internal conflicts into game language.

The Last Report

For its atmosphere of suspense, observation, and exploration of environments filled with quiet tension.

Sally Face / Fran Bow / Dollmare

For their psychological suspense, strangeness, and discomfort created more through atmosphere than direct scares.

A Space for the Unbound / Coffee Talk

For their slow pacing, human relationships, and appreciation for quiet everyday moments.
